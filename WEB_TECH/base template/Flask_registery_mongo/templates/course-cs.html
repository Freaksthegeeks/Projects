Week 1: Foundations of Cybersecurity

Introduction to Cybersecurity

Definition: Cybersecurity refers to the practice of defending computers, servers, mobile devices, electronic systems, networks, and data from malicious attacks.

Scope: The scope of cybersecurity has broadened due to digital transformation, covering areas from individual data protection to national security infrastructure.

Historical Perspective: Tracing the origins of cybersecurity helps understand its evolution, from basic virus protection to complex strategies against advanced persistent threats (APTs).

Types of Cyber Threats

Malware: Malware includes viruses, worms, trojans, spyware, adware, and ransomware. Each of these types targets systems differently, with specific tactics for exploitation.

Phishing: Phishing tricks users into revealing sensitive information, typically through email impersonation. Modern phishing tactics also extend to SMS (smishing) and phone calls (vishing).

Ransomware: Locks data or systems until a ransom is paid. Notable cases, like WannaCry, have shown the devastating potential of ransomware on a global scale.

Social Engineering: Social engineering manipulates individuals into compromising security, often by exploiting human psychology (e.g., urgency, authority).

Core Principles of Cybersecurity

Confidentiality: Ensures that sensitive information is accessible only to authorized users.

Example: Confidentiality is critical in fields like healthcare, where patient data needs secure access controls.

Integrity: Maintains data consistency and prevents unauthorized modifications.

Example: A breach in data integrity in financial systems could lead to substantial financial inaccuracies.

Availability: Guarantees that data and resources are accessible when required by legitimate users, often through redundancy and disaster recovery measures.

Cybersecurity Risk Management

Risk Identification: Recognizing threats and vulnerabilities, such as outdated software that lacks security patches.

Impact Analysis: Understanding the consequences of potential threats to prioritize resources.

Mitigation Strategies: Implementing proactive measures, such as regular software updates, to manage and reduce risks.

Week 2: Network Security

Network Security Fundamentals

Definition: Network security protects data integrity, confidentiality, and availability as it travels over networks.

Tools & Technologies: Essential tools include firewalls, Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS), and Virtual Private Networks (VPNs).

Firewalls: Control incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules, forming a barrier between trusted and untrusted networks.

Network Segmentation and Architecture

Network Segmentation: Dividing a network into distinct segments helps restrict access and isolate threats.

DMZ (Demilitarized Zone): A DMZ is a subnetwork that adds an extra security layer, often housing public-facing services like web servers without compromising the internal network.

Zero Trust Architecture: The zero-trust model assumes no implicit trust, even within a network perimeter. Access is limited strictly on a need-to-know basis, reducing risks.

Firewalls and IDS/IPS

Types of Firewalls:

Network-based Firewalls: Filter traffic between networks, commonly placed at gateways.

Host-based Firewalls: Installed on individual devices to protect specific hosts.

Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS):

IDS: Detects suspicious activities but does not block them.

IPS: Detects and automatically blocks malicious traffic, mitigating threats in real-time.

Secure Network Protocols

TLS/SSL: Transport Layer Security (TLS) and its predecessor Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) are protocols that encrypt data for secure communication.

HTTPS: Secures data between web browsers and servers, essential for protecting user information like login credentials and credit card numbers.

SSH (Secure Shell): SSH is a secure protocol for accessing remote servers, encrypting the connection and ensuring data integrity.

Week 3: Cryptography and Data Protection

Introduction to Cryptography

Purpose: Cryptography secures communication, ensuring that only authorized parties can understand the transmitted data.

Symmetric and Asymmetric Encryption:

Symmetric: A single key for encryption and decryption, used for data at rest.

Asymmetric: Public-private key pairs used for secure exchanges, enabling secure communications over public channels.

Encryption Algorithms: Common encryption methods include AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) and RSA, which offer strong data protection.

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

PKI Components: PKI relies on digital certificates, Certificate Authorities (CAs), and keys to establish secure communications.

Digital Certificates: Certify the identity of entities, providing trust in digital transactions and preventing impersonation.

Digital Signatures: A digital signature verifies the senderâ€™s authenticity and message integrity, preventing tampering.

Data Encryption in Practice

Encryption at Rest: Encrypts stored data, commonly with AES-256, to prevent unauthorized access.

Encryption in Transit: Encrypts data moving across networks, such as email or web traffic, securing it from interception.

Hash Functions and Password Security

Purpose of Hashing: Hashing transforms data into fixed-size strings, commonly for secure password storage.

Salting and Hashing: Adding a unique salt to hashed passwords strengthens security, as it prevents attackers from using precomputed tables.

Key Hashing Algorithms: SHA-256 is a popular cryptographic hash function, widely used for password protection.

Week 4: Application Security

Application Security Overview

Importance: Application security ensures that software is resilient against attacks, safeguarding sensitive data and user privacy.

Common Vulnerabilities:

Injection Attacks: SQL injection is one of the most common attacks where an attacker inserts malicious code into queries.

Cross-Site Scripting (XSS): Injects malicious scripts into web pages viewed by others, often affecting user interactions.

Broken Authentication: Weak authentication can allow unauthorized access, compromising data security.

Secure Coding Practices

Input Validation: Verifying input data is crucial for preventing injection attacks and ensuring data integrity.

Code Review and Testing: Regular reviews and tests identify security flaws before release, adhering to secure coding standards.

Security Development Lifecycle (SDL): SDL integrates security practices from planning through testing, reducing vulnerabilities in the final product.

Access Controls

RBAC (Role-Based Access Control): Access is limited based on the user's role, ensuring that permissions align with responsibilities.

MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication): MFA strengthens security by requiring two or more factors for user verification.

Least Privilege Principle: Grants the minimum necessary access, reducing potential damage from compromised accounts.

Vulnerability Management

Vulnerability Scanning: Automated tools, like Nessus or OpenVAS, scan systems to detect and report vulnerabilities.

Patch Management: Regular updates and patches fix known vulnerabilities, preventing attackers from exploiting outdated software.

Week 5: Incident Response and Threat Intelligence

Incident Response Process

Preparation: Creating and testing an incident response plan (IRP) ensures a structured approach to cybersecurity incidents.

Detection and Analysis: Monitoring systems to identify suspicious activities, assessing the nature and extent of incidents.

Containment: Immediate actions, such as isolating affected devices, prevent the threat from spreading.

Eradication and Recovery

Eradication: Eliminating the threat from the environment, such as removing malware or closing vulnerabilities.

Recovery: Ensuring systems are safe before resuming normal operations, validating that security controls are functioning correctly.

Post-Incident Review

Learning from Incidents: Analyzing what went wrong, how it was handled, and identifying improvements.

Documentation: Thorough documentation of the response process and outcomes helps refine future actions.

Threat Intelligence

Role of Threat Intelligence: Provides insights into potential threats, helping to anticipate and defend against attacks.

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs): IoCs like IP addresses, file hashes, and URLs signal suspicious activities, guiding proactive security actions.

Types of Threat Intelligence: Strategic intelligence addresses overall trends, while technical intelligence focuses on specific threats.
